AI Governance Framework

Role Taxonomy & Decision Architecture

The platform distinguishes between three categories of participant — each with a distinct relationship to the AI system being governed. The implementer role is deliberately broad: it covers whoever is bringing an AI system into the organization, regardless of whether they built it, bought it, or enabled it.

Im
Implementer
The person or team bringing an AI system into the organization. Registers the system. Drives it through the lifecycle.
Who this covers
Builder An engineer or data scientist who developed the system internally. Has deep technical knowledge of the model. Completes the technical fields of the intake record directly.
Procurer An HR manager, operations lead, or business unit owner who selected and is deploying a third-party vendor tool — a hiring platform, a performance system, an AI assistant. May not know the model's internals. The platform surfaces different intake questions based on this context.
Enabler An IT administrator or department head who is turning on a capability that already exists in an approved platform — enabling Gemini across Google Workspace, activating Copilot in Microsoft 365, enabling AI features in an existing SaaS tool. The system already exists; what's being governed is the organizational deployment decision.
What the platform does differently for each

At intake, the platform asks "how is this system coming into the organization?" — built, bought, or enabled. The answer routes the implementer to a version of the intake form calibrated to what they can actually know. A procurer cannot tell you the training data source; they can tell you what the vendor's data processing agreement says and whether a third-party audit exists. An enabler cannot tell you the model architecture; they can tell you what data the feature will touch and what the vendor's privacy controls are. The governance obligation doesn't disappear — it gets reframed around what the implementer holds.

Rv
Reviewers
The teams whose knowledge governs whether a system is fit for deployment. Unchanged from the earlier model — but now reviewing systems brought in by a wider range of implementers.
Legal & compliance

Reviews data authorization, regulatory exposure, vendor contract terms, and jurisdictional requirements. For a procurer deploying a hiring tool, legal's role expands significantly — the vendor contract and DPA become primary governance artifacts.

Ethics & privacy

Reviews bias exposure, fairness implications, PIA, and community impact. For an HR procurer enabling a hiring system, the ethics reviewer may need to request vendor bias audits as a condition of approval — even if the procurer doesn't have that documentation at intake.

Technical reviewer

For procured or enabled systems, this role may be filled by IT security or a technical lead who didn't build the system but can assess integration risks, data flows, and access controls. Separates "technical knowledge of the model" from "technical knowledge of the deployment."

St
Stakeholders
People with a legitimate interest in the system's governance but without a decision-making or review role in the platform.
Accountable owner

Holds final deployment authority. In the HR hiring system scenario, this may be the CHRO or VP of Talent — not the HR business partner who procured the tool.

Community liaison

Represents or is in contact with affected populations. For a company-wide Gemini rollout, this may include employee representatives or a DEI lead who can speak to workforce impact.

Executive / board

Receives aggregated risk posture reports. Does not participate in individual system reviews — sees patterns across the registry, open blocks, and override history.

The decision tree now branches on implementer type before anything else — because what the platform needs to ask, and who it needs to involve, depends entirely on how the system is arriving in the organization.

Select an implementer type to see how the intake form adapts — same governance obligation, different questions calibrated to what each implementer can actually know.
